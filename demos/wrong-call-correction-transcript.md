# Demo: The wrong strategic call - and how the skill catches it

This transcript is didactic. It shows a user who frames their situation incorrectly, receives an
initial analysis that follows the wrong framing, then walks through the correction when the skill
forces the game-type classification step. The situation: a SaaS founder negotiating a contract
renewal with their largest customer (40% of revenue).

The instructive failure is Phase 1 misclassification. The skill initially follows the user's framing
into a cooperative repeated-game analysis. Then the Phase 1 gate catches it.

---

**User:** I need to renegotiate my annual contract with our biggest customer. They represent about
40% of our revenue. Their procurement lead just told me they want a 15% price reduction "given the
current economic environment." We've worked with them for three years, great relationship. I want to
handle this well without damaging the trust we've built.

**Agent:** Before I begin the analysis: what is your current annual contract value with this
customer, and when does the contract expire?

---

**User:** It is £480K per year. Contract expires in 6 weeks. We have not started formal renewal
talks yet - this request came informally from their procurement lead.

**Agent:** Phase 1: Deconstruction.

[INITIAL - INCORRECT FRAMING]

Game type: repeated, indefinite. Three-year relationship, ongoing dependency on both sides, high
switching costs for both parties. Cooperative logic applies. Relationship preservation is a primary
variable, not a secondary one.

This looks like a signalling game with a price anchoring move on their side. Procurement leads
routinely open with a reduction request as institutional mandate; it does not necessarily reflect
the decision-maker's true position. The face-saving structure matters here.

My initial read: the optimal play is to acknowledge the request, reframe around value delivered in
year three, and offer a modest concession (5-8%) packaged with an expanded contract term.

---

**Agent:** Wait. Before I proceed to Phase 2, I need to complete the Phase 1 gate.

**Hard classification required.** This situation has a feature I have not accounted for: the
contract expires in 6 weeks. That changes everything.

**Revised game type: repeated, finite - with endpoint.**

This is not an indefinite repeated game. There is a known endpoint in 6 weeks. In finite repeated
games, backward induction applies: cooperation unravels near the end. The cooperative payoffs that
sustain a three-year relationship do not apply in the same way when the horizon is 6 weeks and the
other side knows it.

The procurement lead's timing is not coincidental. An informal price reduction request 6 weeks
before expiry, without formal renewal talks initiated, is a position-setting move designed to anchor
the renewal negotiation before it formally begins. They are using the countdown as pressure. The
"great relationship" framing the user has internalised is doing work for the counterpart here, not
for the user.

**I am revising the initial read. The cooperative reframe-and-concede approach was wrong.**

Confirm this classification before I proceed: has your counterpart given any indication they are
considering alternatives, or is there any signal they have spoken to your competitors?

---

**User:** Actually, yes - their procurement lead mentioned they had "reviewed the market" as part of
their cost review. I took it as a negotiating phrase but I am not sure.

**Agent:** Phase 2: Incentive Mapping (revised).

**"Reviewed the market" is a material signal, not a phrase.** It means one of two things: they have
a competing quote (real BATNA) or they want you to believe they do (bluff). Either way, the
statement was made deliberately. You need to know which.

**Their position:**
- Public: want 15% reduction, citing economic environment
- True priority ranking (most likely): (1) cost reduction, (2) continuity, (3) relationship
- The "great relationship" you reference is real but it is a switching cost argument for them too.
  Three years of integration and onboarding is expensive to replicate. Their mention of market
  review may be bluff precisely because the real cost of switching is high.

**Your position:**
- Real BATNA: a 40% revenue dependency means your BATNA is weak if they leave. This is the
  critical asymmetry. Do not reveal it.
- Your real power: switching costs on their side (integration depth, team familiarity, three years
  of workflow adaptation). These are real and you should quantify them before Phase 5.

**Decision-making unit:** Procurement lead is not the decision-maker. Who signs? What does the
budget holder care about that procurement does not? Risk reduction, continuity, avoiding a
migration project in Q4 are all arguments that bypass procurement's price mandate.

**Phase 1 error acknowledged:** my initial framing treated this as a cooperative game and defaulted
to concession. The finite horizon, the market review signal, and the informal timing of the request
are all features that push the analysis toward a harder, more asymmetric play. The concession
strategy would have landed you at a 15% cut with no structural protection. That is the wrong call.

---

**Phase 3: Strategy Space.**

Available moves (non-dominated):

1. **Counter-anchor with value justification.** Respond to the informal request formally. Quantify
   the cost of switching: integration hours, onboarding, workflow disruption, historical support
   response time. Frame the current price as already below market for your support tier. Offer 0%
   reduction with a 12-month contract term extension.

2. **Segmented concession with expansion.** Offer 8% reduction on the existing scope if they commit
   to an expanded scope at list price (new users, new module, extended term). The blended effect is
   price-neutral to you; they see a reduction number. This is the face-saving structure.

3. **Escalate to the real decision-maker.** Bypass procurement directly by requesting a business
   review with their VP or CTO before formal renewal. Agenda: value delivered in year three. This
   moves the conversation from a price negotiation to a strategic review. Procurement's 15% mandate
   becomes irrelevant if the decision-maker is not anchored to it.

**Dominated move (eliminated):** Conceding 15% without a structural exchange. Gives them the
number, signals your BATNA is weak, and sets the anchor for next year's renewal.

---

**Phase 4: Equilibrium.**

If they have a real competing quote: their outside option gives them genuine bargaining power. The question
is whether the switching cost exceeds the 15% saving over the contract term. At £480K, 15% is £72K.
A full migration project is typically 3-6x that in internal hours and disruption. If their switching
cost exceeds £72K (likely), their BATNA is worse than they are presenting.

If the market review is a bluff: they have no outside option and the 15% request is pure position.
Your hold is dominant.

In both cases, the equilibrium is the same: you do not concede 15% without an exchange. The
difference is in how hard you hold.

---

**Phase 5: Recommendation.**

Execute move 3 first. Request a business review with the decision-maker above procurement this
week. Frame it as a standard annual review. Do not mention the price request - that conversation
has not officially happened.

In that meeting: lead with value delivered (quantified). Introduce the renewal conversation as
something you want to get ahead of together. Ask what their priorities are for next year. Let them
surface the price sensitivity - do not volunteer it.

If procurement's 15% request comes up: "We are happy to look at how the commercial structure
can evolve as your use expands. Our standard renewal is [X]. If there is appetite to expand the
scope, we can look at what that does to the blended rate."

Commitment device: send a formal renewal proposal before they send theirs. Whoever anchors the
formal proposal controls the reference point.

Contingency: if they come back with a hard 15% demand and a competing quote, ask to see it. Not
aggressively - as a signal that you take it seriously. In most cases, a competing quote that is
real will be shown. A bluff will not.

---

**Phase 6: Dynamic Adaptation.**

The three-year relationship is an asset, not an obligation to concede. In the repeated game that
follows this renewal, the precedent set here determines the anchor for every future renegotiation.
A 15% concession becomes the floor for next year's ask. A held position with a structured
alternative sets a different precedent.

If this customer is 40% of revenue, the medium-term play is to reduce that concentration before
the next renewal. Not because this customer is a threat, but because your BATNA in this negotiation
was too weak. That weakness is structural.

---

**Debrief: what the initial analysis got wrong**

The first Phase 1 read classified this as a cooperative repeated game and defaulted to
concession logic. Two features should have triggered a reclassification immediately:

1. **Finite horizon (6 weeks to expiry).** Backward induction applies. Cooperative strategies
   unravel as the endpoint approaches. The relationship argument is weaker here than it would be
   mid-contract.

2. **Informal timing of the request.** A formal price negotiation arrives in a renewal proposal.
   An informal request 6 weeks before expiry is position-setting, not negotiation. Treating it as
   negotiation concedes the frame.

The corrected analysis produces a materially different recommendation: no concession without an
exchange, bypass procurement, control the formal anchor. The initial wrong call would have cost
approximately £72K annually and set a precedent for every subsequent renewal.

The Phase 1 gate exists precisely to catch this class of error. When in doubt about whether a
situation has a finite horizon, assume it does and test the cooperative logic against that
assumption before proceeding.
