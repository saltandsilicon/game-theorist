# Worked Examples

Fourteen real-world situations run through the six-phase model. Each follows the canonical format:
situation, stakes, your position, their position, power balance, and play.

---

## Example: Salary negotiation at a late-stage startup

**Situation:** You are a senior engineer being recruited by a Series C startup (200 employees, $40M
ARR). They have verbally offered $180K base. You are currently at $165K with $120K in unvested
equity vesting over 24 months. The recruiter says the offer is "pretty much at band."

**Stakes:** For you: $15K annual delta, plus the unvested equity you forfeit if you leave early
(approximately $60K in present value). For them: a 3-6 month search if you decline, a likely
counter-offer loop, and a role that has been open for 11 weeks.

**Your position:** BATNA is staying put. You are comfortable, well-regarded, and
the equity vests cleanly. Choosing not to take the new role is uncomfortable; it is not a financial risk. Time
pressure is low on your side. Constraints: you need at least $185K to make the move financially
neutral on an annualised basis (equity replacement cost plus risk).

**Their position:** BATNA is reopening the search. The role has been open 11 weeks, which signals either high standards or difficulty closing candidates. Either way, their cost of a miss
is high. They said "at band" but bands at Series C companies are typically $30-40K wide. The key
constraint is not the band, it is their internal equity among peers.

**Power balance:** You have two forms: (1) a real, concrete BATNA (current role, unvested equity); (2) information. You know the role has been open 11 weeks. They do not know you know. Information
asymmetry favours you. They do not know your reservation price.

**Play:** Anchor above your reservation price, then quantify the equity you are leaving behind. Do
not ask for a higher salary. Ask them to make you whole on the unvested equity. This reframes the
negotiation from "you want more" to "they are asking you to absorb a cost." Specific language: "I
am genuinely excited about this role. The base is close. My main constraint is that I am leaving
$60K in unvested equity on the table. I would need either a signing bonus or an accelerated vest
to make the transition financially clean." Then stop talking. Silence. Their next move reveals
whether the band story was true or tactical. If they counter below your number, agree to a 90-day
review tied to a specific milestone. This gives them a face-saving path and you a credible
commitment device for a mid-year raise.

## Example: Pricing war, B2B SaaS crowded category

**Situation:** You run a project management SaaS at $45/seat/month. Two competitors have dropped to
$25 and $30 respectively over the past quarter. You are at 92% gross retention, they are acquiring
fast at low price. Your primary customer is mid-market (50-500 seats). Both competitors came from
the SMB segment and their feature set reflects it.

**Stakes:** If you hold price, you risk losing price-sensitive evaluation cycles. If you drop, you
compress margin on 4,200 existing seats ($2.27M ARR impact) and signal that your price was
arbitrary. Your net revenue retention is 118%, theirs is unknown but likely lower given their
customer profile.

**Your position:** BATNA is holding price and accepting slower new logo growth. You have 92%
retention and 118% NRR, meaning existing customers are expanding. Your cost of staying at $45 is
slower acquisition, not churn. Time horizon: long. Constraints: you cannot reduce price for existing
customers without destroying trust and margin simultaneously.

**Their position:** BATNA is continuing to burn cash on CAC at $25 and hoping to reach scale before
funding runs dry. Their game is a land-grab for logo count, likely tied to a fundraising narrative.
Time horizon: short (next funding round). Their stated objective is "democratising project
management." Their true objective is ACV growth to hit metrics for Series A or B.

**Power balance:** You control the mid-market feature set they do not have. You have a reference customer
base they cannot replicate with SMB logos. Your customers are harder to displace (deeper
integrations, more seats, more process dependency). You have time. They do not.

**Play:** Do not compete on price at all. Launch a mid-market certification programme or a
dedicated onboarding track that SMB tools cannot credibly offer. This raises the switching cost for
your segment without touching price. Then publish a "total cost of ownership" comparison that
includes migration cost, retraining, and integration rebuild. Quantify what switching to a
$25/seat tool costs a 200-seat company. You are changing the unit of competition from
price-per-seat to cost-of-change. If a prospect is clearly price-anchored and going to the $25
option regardless, let them. Do not chase the segment your competitors are optimised for.

## Example: Fundraising, seed round, two term sheets

**Situation:** You are raising a $1.5M pre-seed. Angel syndicate A offered $1.5M at $6M pre with a
2x liquidation preference. Angel syndicate B offered $1.2M at $7M pre, no liquidation preference,
pro-rata rights. You need to close in 30 days for payroll coverage.

**Stakes:** The liquidation preference on A's term means that if you exit at under $12M, A gets
paid first and you get less than your equity percentage suggests. B's terms are cleaner but the
lower amount creates a $300K gap. Your burn is $60K/month; you have 45 days of runway.

**Your position:** BATNA is bridge financing from an existing angel at punishing terms (20%
discount, no cap). Time pressure is real: 30 days or bridge. Constraints: you cannot extend the
process past 45 days without hitting payroll. You need to close, but you need to close on terms you
can live with through a Series A.

**Their position:** A: their liquidation preference is a downside protection mechanism, not a
negotiating tactic. They have seen enough seed deals fail to price in risk this way. Their BATNA is
the next deal in their pipeline. B: they offered clean terms but less capital. Their BATNA is also
the next deal. B's pro-rata rights signal they expect to follow on. They believe in the upside.

**Power balance:** You have two term sheets, which creates a competitive dynamic even if mild. The
information asymmetry is critical: they do not know your runway. You know theirs is not urgent. The
30-day deadline, if you reveal it, weakens you. Do not reveal it.

**Play:** Go back to B. Tell them you are leaning toward their terms because of the cleaner
structure but need the full $1.5M to avoid a bridge round. Ask if they can increase the check or
bring in a co-investor. If B cannot reach $1.5M, go back to A and negotiate the liquidation
preference: ask for 1x non-participating instead of 2x. Frame it as a standard-market term, not a
concession request. "Most pre-seed rounds we see are at 1x non-participating. Can we align to
market?" This is face-saving: they are not backing down, they are meeting market norms. If A holds
the 2x, take B's deal and immediately start working a bridge from angels in your network for the
$300K gap. Two smaller checks are better than one bad liquidation preference that follows you to
exit.

## Example: Co-founder equity split, revisited at Series A

**Situation:** Two co-founders (technical and commercial) split equity 50/50 at founding. Two years
in, the technical co-founder has built the entire product and manages a team of 4 engineers. The
commercial co-founder closed the first 12 customers but has been less effective since, contributing
roughly 20% of what the technical founder contributes by any reasonable measure. A Series A investor
is asking about co-founder dynamics before committing.

**Stakes:** For the technical co-founder: significant undercompensation relative to contribution. For
the commercial co-founder: losing equity that has been accruing for two years. For the company: an
unstable founding team is a red flag that can kill the Series A. The investor has implicitly made
the deal conditional on co-founder alignment.

**Your position (technical co-founder):** Your BATNA is raising the issue with the investor directly
(nuclear option, kills the deal and the relationship) or continuing with the inequity (acceptable
only if your equity vests fully in 12 months). You are the harder-to-replace founder at this stage.
Your constraint: you cannot be seen as destabilising the round.

**Their position (commercial co-founder):** BATNA is losing equity in a renegotiation or losing the
partner relationship entirely. Their power sits in the existing cap table and the timing: any
renegotiation now creates noise before a close. They know you need them to co-sign the round.

**Power balance:** The investor's question works in your favour without you raising the topic
yourself. The investor has already surfaced the fault line. You can channel that externally created
pressure rather than initiating conflict.

**Play:** Do not renegotiate equity directly. Instead, propose a role restructuring that changes the
vesting profile going forward. "Given where we are in the business, let's talk about whether the
roles still map to the founding structure." Introduce a performance-based equity grant for the
technical team and a new commercial hire that effectively dilutes the commercial co-founder's
influence (not equity, influence). If the commercial co-founder resists any restructuring, raise it
with the investor as a governance issue, not a personal one: "We are working through a role
evolution as we scale. We can walk you through our thinking." This keeps the round alive and puts
the resolution timeline after the close, where you have more options. The Shapley value here is
clear: compute each co-founder's marginal contribution to each major outcome. Use that as the
analytical basis for the restructuring conversation, not feelings.

## Example: Market entry, crowded category, well-funded incumbents

**Situation:** You are launching a developer tools product into a category with three funded
incumbents (a $200M company, a $50M company, and a $15M company). The $200M player owns 60% of
the market. Your differentiator is a local-first architecture (data never leaves the developer's
machine). The $200M player has recently had a security incident.

**Stakes:** If you enter frontally, you compete for the same buyers the incumbents are actively
defending. You have 18 months of runway. If you do not find a wedge, you burn out competing for the
middle of the market. The security incident is a real but temporary opportunity.

**Your position:** BATNA is staying in beta and iterating (safe but slow). You have a technical
moat (local-first) that none of the incumbents can replicate quickly. Their architectures are
cloud-native and a rewrite would take 12-18 months. Time horizon: 18 months of runway but a
durable architectural advantage. Constraints: no sales team, no brand, no reference customers.

**Their position:** The $200M player's BATNA is doing nothing. They own 60% of the market and any
response to you validates your threat. If they lower price or copy your architecture, they signal
weakness to their existing customers. The $50M and $15M players' BATNA is competing for the same
middle as before. None of them can make the security guarantee you can without a full rewrite.

**Power balance:** The security incident has created a specific buyer segment: enterprise teams with
heightened compliance requirements post-incident. This segment is not your competitors' core. It is a new buyer type your architecture uniquely addresses. You have a six-month window before the
$200M player's incident fades from memory and before any of them can credibly claim to fix the
underlying architecture.

**Play:** Stackelberg entry. Move first into the compliance-sensitive enterprise segment before any
competitor positions there. Do not compete on the incumbent's terms (features, integrations,
support). Compete on the one axis they cannot: data residency guarantees. Write a technical white
paper on local-first security architecture and distribute it to the CISO and DevSecOps community.
This creates the buyer category before you have a product they can evaluate. The $200M player
cannot respond without drawing more attention to their incident. The $50M and $15M players lack the
technical credibility to copy the message. You are not entering their market. You are creating a
segment adjacent to it where you are the only option.

## Example: Partnership negotiation, unequal power

**Situation:** You are a $2M ARR startup negotiating a go-to-market partnership with a $500M ARR
enterprise software company. They want to resell your product to their customer base (8,000
enterprise accounts). They proposed a standard reseller agreement: 40% margin, minimum commitment of
zero, 12-month exclusivity in their vertical.

**Stakes:** 40% margin means you earn $60 on a $100 sale where you do your own support, docs, and
onboarding. Zero minimum commitment means they can park you and never activate. 12-month
exclusivity blocks you from building other channels in the vertical, the one area where their
customer base is largest.

**Your position:** BATNA is direct sales into the vertical (slower, more expensive, but no lock-in).
You have 18 months of runway. The partnership could 3-5x your distribution if activated. The risk
is that it never gets activated and the exclusivity clause strangles the direct channel for a year.

**Their position:** BATNA is using a competitor's product or building it internally (costly). They
came to you, which signals your product has something they need. Their internal champion is a VP
who needs this partnership to close before their Q3 budget cycle. The procurement team (not the
champion) is enforcing the standard reseller terms. This is a coalition problem: the VP needs the
deal, procurement is blocking on price.

**Power balance:** You have three forms. Product: they need what you have. Timing: the VP's Q3 deadline is real. Coalition: the internal champion is motivated in ways procurement is not.

**Play:** Map the decision-making unit before responding to the term sheet. Contact the VP champion
directly (not procurement) with a counter-framed around their Q3 outcome: "We want to make this
work for your Q3 deadline. Here is a structure that we can close quickly." Then propose: 25% margin
(not 40%), minimum $200K commitment in year one, exclusivity scoped to two named sub-segments (not
the full vertical), with a performance ratchet that drops your margin to 20% if they hit $500K.
This gives procurement a number to anchor (25% vs 40%, a "win"), gives the VP a commitment that
activates the channel, and gives you a non-exclusive path in the broader vertical. The performance
ratchet makes the deal feel like a partnership, not a vendor transaction.

## Example: Supplier renegotiation, single-source dependency

**Situation:** You have one supplier for a critical component (a specialised microcontroller). Your
contract is up for renewal. The supplier has raised unit costs 22% and is asking for a 3-year
lock-in at the new rate. You buy $800K/year from them. They represent roughly 35% of your component
COGS. You have found one alternative supplier but their lead time is 14 weeks and their quality
certification is not yet complete.

**Stakes:** Accepting the 22% increase locks in $176K of additional annual cost. A 3-year lock-in
means $528K in excess cost over the period if pricing stays elevated. Refusing risks supply
disruption on a single-source component. The alternative supplier is real but not yet ready.

**Your position:** BATNA is the alternative supplier, but it is weak today: 14-week lead time, incomplete certification. It becomes strong in roughly 60-90 days when certification completes. Time pressure is the key variable. Delay the negotiation by 90 days and your BATNA transforms. Constraints: you cannot reveal that the alternative
supplier exists or that you are actively qualifying them.

**Their position:** BATNA is losing your $800K/year account. At 35% of your COGS, you are probably
a meaningful customer for them. Their 22% increase may reflect real cost increases (component
inflation, energy costs) or may be a test of your price sensitivity. The 3-year lock-in is a signal
that they are uncertain about their own future pricing and want to lock in revenue.

**Power balance:** They do not know about the alternative supplier. Time is on your side if you can
delay. The 3-year lock-in request works against them: it reveals they need the certainty more
than they need the margin.

**Play:** Open by questioning the 22% increase with specifics: ask for a cost breakdown
(materials, labour, logistics). Not to win the argument. To buy time and signal
that you are not going to absorb the increase without scrutiny. Then counter-propose: accept 8%
increase, two-year contract (not three), with a price-adjustment clause tied to a commodity index
(e.g., copper futures if that is relevant). The commodity index clause is a commitment device for
both sides. It removes future pricing disputes by tying cost to an external reference. Meanwhile,
accelerate the alternative supplier qualification. Never negotiate a supply contract without at
least one visible alternative, even if it is not yet ready. Revealing that you are
evaluating alternatives, without naming them, shifts the balance of power immediately.

## Example: Exit negotiation, acquirer under time pressure

**Situation:** A strategic acquirer (public company, $4B market cap) has approached you with an
informal acquisition offer of $22M for your $3.5M ARR SaaS. You have not run a process. The acquirer's
M&A team said they want to "move quickly." Their Q2 ends in six weeks. Your investors want at
least $28M (8x ARR). You have 24 months of runway and no immediate pressure to sell.

**Stakes:** $6M gap between their informal offer and your investors' floor. The acquirer's Q2
deadline creates real time pressure on their side: a deal that closes in Q2 affects their reported
metrics and integration timeline. Missing Q2 means pushing to Q4 (their Q3 is typically a blackout
for M&A given earnings season).

**Your position:** BATNA is not selling. You have 24 months of runway and a growing business. You can
genuinely walk. Your exit is optional. Theirs, if they want Q2, is not. Constraints: you cannot string them along indefinitely without damaging the relationship and
your reputation in the acquirer's network.

**Their position:** BATNA is acquiring a different company or missing Q2. The "move quickly"
language is the signal. It reveals time pressure. Their true objective is closing in Q2, not
maximising the purchase price. The M&A team is accountable to a deadline, not a price.

**Power balance:** You have time. They do not. Their Q2 deadline is a hard constraint you can use
without explicitly acknowledging it. The gap is $6M on a deal that probably has $50-100M of
strategic value to them. Structuring the delta as an earnout is a standard face-saving mechanism
for both sides.

**Play:** Do not respond to the informal offer with a counter-offer. Respond with process:
"We are flattered and open to exploring this seriously. To do that properly, we would need to run
a structured conversation over the next four to five weeks, which I think aligns with your
timeline." This resets the frame from "negotiating their offer" to "you deciding whether to sell."
It also signals that you are not desperate. Then, privately, begin a conversation with one other
potential acquirer. This is to have a credible alternative you can reference. When the
M&A team comes back with a formal offer, respond with $30M, supported by ARR growth rate and NRR
(beyond only ARR multiple). Frame the $30M as the number that works for your investors and board, not
the number you invented. If they cannot reach $30M in cash, propose a hybrid: $25M cash plus an
earnout of up to $5M tied to ARR at 12 months post-acquisition. The earnout is not a concession. It is a risk-sharing device. You only get the extra $5M if the product you sold them performs.
That is a credible signal to them that you believe in the business.

## Example: Information cascade, enterprise buyer herding

**Situation:** You sell a $90K/year data platform into mid-market. Over six weeks, three prospects
in the same vertical went dark after one well-known early adopter publicly posted that they were
"re-evaluating" your product. None of the three ran their own evaluation to completion. Each cited
"what we are hearing in the market."

**Stakes:** Four deals worth $360K ARR are stalling on a signal that traces back to one account, not
to independent assessment. If the cascade holds, the vertical closes to you for two or three
quarters. If you break it, the pipeline reopens.

**Your position:** BATNA is working other verticals while this one cools. Your product data is
strong: 94% retention in this exact segment, two reference customers with quantified ROI. Your
constraint is that you cannot publicly contradict the vocal account without looking defensive.

**Their position:** The three silent prospects have stated objective "due diligence" and true
objective "do not be the person who picked the tool everyone is moving away from." Their BATNA is a
safer-looking incumbent. They are weighting the observed behaviour of one peer far above their own
half-finished evaluations.

**Power balance:** The cascade is empirically weaker than it looks. One account's churn signal is
being read as a vertical-wide verdict. You hold private information the herd does not: the retention
number and two ROI references. The vocal account's complaint is also narrower than its reach
suggests.

**Play:** Break the cascade with a credible private signal rather than a public rebuttal. Take each
silent prospect a specific, verifiable data point the herd does not have: a reference call with a
named peer at similar scale, plus the segment retention figure. "I know you have heard noise. Here
is the account that looks like yours, and here is what their numbers did." You are not
arguing with the cascade. You are giving each buyer the independent signal that lets them step out
of it without losing face. If the vocal account had a real product gap, name it and show the fix
timeline. A cascade breaks the moment one follower acts on private information instead of the queue.

## Example: Reputational lock-in, founder afraid to pivot on a lead investor

**Situation:** You raised £2M from a prominent VC who championed your original product vision in
their public portfolio posts and an industry talk. Six months in, the market shifted and a sharper
pivot is obvious to you. You are holding back because your lead investor is publicly attached to
the original story.

**Stakes:** Continuing the original plan burns 9 months of runway on a strategy you no longer
believe in. Pivoting risks, in your head, embarrassing the investor who vouched for you and souring
your Series A support. The real exposure is building the wrong product to protect someone else's
public position.

**Your position:** BATNA is executing the pivot with or without enthusiastic investor backing; you
control the roadmap. Your constraint is the belief that the VC values saving face over returns.
Time horizon: the pivot window closes as competitors move.

**Their position:** The VC's stated attachment is to the original vision. Their true objective is a
fund-returning outcome, not a consistent narrative. A reputational investor gains from backing
founders who adapt to evidence. Their reputational damage comes from defending dead strategies in
public, so a well-argued pivot protects them more than stubborn loyalty does.

**Power balance:** You have misread the constraint. You assumed the public commitment binds the VC;
it binds only if the pivot looks like panic. You hold the evidence and the founder mandate. The
investor holds capital and follow-on signalling. Aligned incentives are closer than the surface
suggests.

**Play:** Bring the pivot as a data-backed decision, with the face-saving frame built in. Give the
VC the off-ramp that makes the new direction continuous with the old: "The thesis you backed was
that this market wanted X. The data says the same buyers want X delivered as Y. Same bet, sharper
execution." This lets the investor update in public without recanting. Pre-brief them before any
board or LP exposure so they are never surprised. If they resist, ask which specific metric would
change their mind, and go get it. You convert a feared reputational lock into a shared decision.

## Example: Regulatory framing shift, competitor changes the rules

**Situation:** You compete in industrial logistics on price and speed and you are winning. A weaker
rival who cannot match your unit economics has started lobbying a regulator to mandate a
certification you do not yet hold and they do. The fight is moving from the market to the rulebook.

**Stakes:** If the certification becomes mandatory before you hold it, your speed and price
advantage is frozen out of regulated contracts overnight. The rival converts a losing position on
the merits into a winning one on the framework. The value at risk is the regulated segment, roughly
40% of the addressable market.

**Your position:** BATNA is competing only in unregulated segments while you pursue certification,
which cedes the rival a protected base. Your constraint is lead time: certification takes 7 to 11
months. Your advantage on price and speed is real but only inside the current rules.

**Their position:** The rival's stated objective is "raising industry standards." Their true
objective is changing the game's structure because they cannot win its current version. Their BATNA
is slow decline. The regulatory lever is their highest-value unexercised move, and absence of use so
far is not evidence they will not pull it.

**Power balance:** You have been treating the regulatory boundary as fixed and competing inside it.
The rival treats it as a strategy. As long as you ignore the rulebook as a contested surface, they
choose the field. Your counter-power: customer relationships, the merit case, and your own
ability to shape the standard.

**Play:** Treat the regulatory framing as a live strategy for both sides, not a backdrop. Start
certification now, in parallel, so the rival's lever loses its bite. Then engage the standard
itself: bring data to the regulator and your customers showing that the proposed certification
measures the wrong thing, and propose an outcome-based standard your speed and price advantage
already satisfies. Mobilise the customers who benefit from your model as a coalition with the
regulator. You are not fighting the rule. You are competing to write it. Do not assume the rival
will not escalate just because they have not yet.

## Example: Mutual escalation lock, public pricing and patent standoff

**Situation:** You and a direct competitor have each made loud, credible public commitments. You
announced you will undercut their flagship on price indefinitely; they announced a patent suit
against your core feature. Both threats are real, costly, and aimed to deter. If both execute, you
gut your own margin and they spend two years and millions on litigation that may invalidate their
own patent.

**Stakes:** Holding firm destroys value on both sides. Your price war compresses margin across your
whole base, not only the contested product. Their suit risks their patent and burns cash. Customers
and press are watching, so backing down looks like losing.

**Your position:** BATNA to escalation is a negotiated truce that neither side has proposed because
proposing it first looks weak. Your constraint is the public commitment to undercut. Time horizon:
the margin damage compounds monthly.

**Their position:** Their public commitment is the suit. Their true objective is deterrence, not
mutual destruction. If both execute simultaneously, neither wins: you bleed margin, they risk the
patent. They are locked the same way you are, each waiting for the other to look weak first.

**Power balance:** This is a mutual escalation lock, not a test of resolve. The diagnostic: if both
threats fire at once, who wins? Neither. That makes unilateral conditional de-escalation the
dominant move, and the first mover who de-escalates from confidence can extract a concession because
they hand the other side relief they cannot ask for.

**Play:** Move first, unilaterally, framed as strength. Withdraw the indefinite-undercut commitment
with a stated condition attached: "We are confident enough in our product that we are ending the
blanket price campaign and will compete on value. We expect this clears the way to resolve the IP
question directly rather than in court. If it does not, we reserve every option." Unilateral in
form, conditional in language, framed as confidence not retreat. This gives them a face-saving path
to drop the suit without appearing to fold. A lock is the one situation where the side that backs
down first gains the upper hand.

## Example: Sunk cost commitment trap, doubling down on a failing build

**Situation:** Your team has spent £2M and 18 months building a multiplayer game. Playtests show the
core loop is broken: players churn after three hours. A competitor just launched a similar title to
strong reviews. Leadership wants to commit another £800K to marketing and polish "to recoup what we
have spent."

**Stakes:** The £2M is gone either way. The live decision is whether the next £800K earns a return.
The sunk cost is being used as the argument for the spend, which is the trap. Committing it on weak
forward prospects converts one loss into two.

**Your position:** BATNA is cancelling now and redeploying the £800K and the team to a project with
live prospects, or pivoting the core mechanic with fresh money justified on its own merits. Your
constraint is organisational: the team is emotionally invested and reads cancellation as failure.

**Their position:** Leadership's stated objective is "recoup the investment." Their true objective
is avoiding the visible write-off and the face cost of having backed it. The sunk cost is doing the
talking. Their BATNA, an honest post-mortem, feels worse to them than a gamble that defers the
reckoning.

**Power balance:** The decision-relevant facts favour stopping: broken core loop, a stronger
competitor already shipped, no evidence the £800K fixes retention. The only force pulling the other
way is face, not economics. Whoever reframes the question controls the outcome.

**Play:** Strip the sunk cost out of the decision and give leadership a face-saving frame to stop.
Reframe from "do we recoup £2M" to "does £800K earn its return against this market, starting from
today." Put the forward-only numbers on one page: retention curve, competitor traction, realistic
revenue. Offer the off-ramp that is not a confession: "We learned the core loop the hard way, and
that tells us exactly what to build next. Redeploying now is acting on what this £2M taught us." If
a pivot of the mechanic is viable, fund it as a new bet judged on its own forecast, never as a
rescue of the old one.

## Example: One-shot framing error, treating a repeated game as a single deal

**Situation:** You are negotiating a one-time software licence with another company. The payoff
table looks like a classic prisoner's dilemma: defecting (inflated price, thin support) beats
cooperating if the other side cooperates, and you will, on paper, never deal with them again. Your
team is preparing an aggressive position, assuming the counterpart will do the same.

**Stakes:** If you both defect on a misread of the game as one-shot, you each extract a worse deal
and poison a relationship that is not isolated in practice. The contract value is moderate; the
reputational spillover across a small industry is larger.

**Your position:** BATNA is other vendors, but switching is costly once integrated. Your constraint
is the assumption baked into your prep: that this is a single, isolated interaction with no shadow
of the future.

**Their position:** Their stated objective is best price on this contract. Their true objective, if
they are sophisticated, includes their own reputation in a market where buyers talk. Their BATNA is
also other counterparties. They are likely reading repeated-game incentives even if the contract is
nominally one-shot.

**Power balance:** The "one-shot" label is the error. Business deals sit inside reputation networks,
information-sharing communities, relationship-specific investments, and repeat interactions disguised
as single ones. Reclassify the game and the dominant strategy flips from defect to conditional
cooperate.

**Play:** Reclassify before you set the position. Treat this as repeated-indefinite: defection here
gets priced into every future dealing through reputation, so cooperate first and signal you are
doing so. Open with fair terms and full support, name the longer relationship explicitly ("we expect
to do more than this one contract, and we price accordingly"), and reserve tit-for-tat only if they
defect. You capture the mutual-cooperation payoff the one-shot framing would have thrown away, and
you protect the reputational asset the contract value alone does not capture.
