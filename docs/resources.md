# Resources

Books, papers, and courses that informed this skill. Ordered by accessibility — start at the top if
you're new to game theory.

---

## Books

### Accessible

**The Art of Strategy** — Avinash Dixit & Barry Nalebuff (2008) The best introduction. Game theory
for general audiences — negotiation, pricing, competition, voting. No maths required. Start here.

**Thinking Strategically** — Avinash Dixit & Barry Nalebuff (1991) The original. More detailed than
_The Art of Strategy_, with deeper treatment of the core concepts. Read this after the above.

**Co-opetition** — Adam Brandenburger & Barry Nalebuff (1996) Game theory applied specifically to
business strategy. The "value net" framework: competitors, complementors, customers, suppliers.
Underrated.

**The Evolution of Cooperation** — Robert Axelrod (1984) How cooperation emerges from
self-interested agents in repeated interactions. The tit-for-tat experiments. One of the most
important books in the field.

**Superforecasting** — Philip Tetlock & Dan Gardner (2015) Not pure game theory, but essential for
calibrating probability estimates and avoiding the overconfidence that ruins strategic models.

### Intermediate

**Strategy: An Introduction to Game Theory** — Joel Watson (2002) University-level textbook. Clear
and well-structured. Good if you want to understand the maths without a PhD.

**Game Theory** — Drew Fudenberg & Jean Tirole (1991) The graduate-level reference. Dense but
complete. Skip to relevant chapters — not a cover-to-cover read.

**The Strategy of Conflict** — Thomas Schelling (1960) The book that introduced focal points,
commitment devices, and credible threats. Written during the Cold War. Every concept applies to
business. Essential.

**Micromotives and Macrobehavior** — Thomas Schelling (1978) Tipping points, segregation, and
emergent order from individual decisions. Foundational for thinking about markets and networks.

### Advanced

**A Course in Game Theory** — Osborne & Rubinstein (1994) Free online. Rigorous mathematical
treatment. Reference text for specific theorems.

**Auction Theory** — Vijay Krishna (2009) The standard text on auction design and bidding strategy.
Dense but definitive.

## Papers

**"Equilibrium Points in N-Person Games"** — John Nash (1950) The original Nash equilibrium paper.
Two pages. Read it. https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.36.1.48

**"The Evolution of Cooperation"** — Robert Axelrod & William Hamilton (1981) The Science paper on
tit-for-tat winning the round-robin tournament. Foundational.

**"Job Market Signaling"** — Michael Spence (1973) The signalling paper. Education as a signal of
ability — not as a producer of skill. Changed how we think about credentials, marketing, and costly
commitment.

**"Counterspeculation, Auctions, and Competitive Sealed Tenders"** — William Vickrey (1961) The
second-price auction paper. Truth-telling as dominant strategy.

**"An Experimental Study of Ultimatum Bargaining"** — Güth, Schmittberger & Schwarze (1982) The
ultimatum game experiment. People reject unfair offers — even when it costs them money. The human
variable matters.

## Courses (free)

**Game Theory** — Stanford / Coursera (Matthew Jackson, Kevin Leyton-Brown, Yoav Shoham) The
canonical MOOC. 10 weeks. Covers Nash equilibrium, repeated games, Bayesian games, auctions,
coalitions. https://www.coursera.org/learn/game-theory-1

**Game Theory II: Advanced Applications** — Stanford / Coursera Mechanism design, social choice,
network effects. https://www.coursera.org/learn/game-theory-2

**Algorithmic Game Theory** — Tim Roughgarden (Stanford, lectures on YouTube) Computational
perspective. Price of anarchy, mechanism design, auctions.

## Key concepts — quick reference

| Term                          | Definition                                                                           |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Nash Equilibrium              | Strategy profile where no player benefits from unilateral deviation                  |
| Dominant strategy             | A strategy that is best regardless of what others do                                 |
| BATNA                         | Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — your outside option                     |
| Pareto improvement            | A change that makes at least one player better off and no one worse off              |
| Subgame perfect equilibrium   | Nash equilibrium that requires rational play at every decision node                  |
| Focal point (Schelling point) | A salient solution players converge on without communication                         |
| Commitment device             | An action that removes future flexibility, making a threat or promise credible       |
| Signalling                    | Sending a costly, observable action to convey private information credibly           |
| Winner's curse                | The tendency to overpay in common-value auctions due to optimistic estimation        |
| Shapley value                 | A fair method for distributing coalition surplus based on marginal contributions     |
| Tit-for-tat                   | Cooperate first, then mirror the other player's last move — robust in repeated games |
| Mechanism design              | Designing the rules of the game to produce a desired equilibrium                     |
