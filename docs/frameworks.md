# Frameworks Reference

Eleven core frameworks. Each maps to a class of situation. The skill selects the right one after
Phase 1: Deconstruction.

---

## 1. Zero-sum / constant-sum game

**When**: The total value is fixed. One side's gain is exactly the other's loss. No move makes the
pie bigger, so the only question is who captures which slice.

**The structure**: Pure conflict. Dividing a fixed settlement, splitting a fixed territory, two
bidders for one contract where price is the only term. Cooperation cannot improve the joint outcome,
because there is no joint outcome to improve, only a division.

**Key insight**: Do not waste effort hunting for a win-win that does not exist. In a true zero-sum
game the work is distributive, not creative. But most business situations that look zero-sum are
not. Test it first: can any move grow the pie? If yes, the game is non-zero-sum and a different
framework applies. Treating a non-zero-sum game as zero-sum is the most common strategic error.

**Business examples**:

- Litigation with a fixed settlement pool
- Bidding for a single contract on price alone
- Dividing a fixed equity pool with no new value entering

**What to do**: Improve your relative position, not the joint one. Strengthen your BATNA, control
information, shape the reference point the other side anchors on. Before committing to zero-sum
tactics, double-check the pie is genuinely fixed.

## 2. Prisoner's Dilemma (non-zero-sum)

**When**: Both parties would do better by cooperating, but each has an individual incentive to
defect, and defecting is individually rational whatever the other does.

**The structure**: Each player can cooperate or defect. Mutual cooperation beats mutual defection
for both. But defecting while the other cooperates pays best of all, and being the lone cooperator
pays worst. So both defect, and both end up worse than if they had cooperated. This is not zero-sum:
mutual cooperation is a real, shared gain that the structure of the game destroys.

**Key insight**: Rational individual behaviour produces a collectively worse outcome. The Nash
equilibrium (both defect) is stable but Pareto-inferior. The gain from cooperation is real and
shared, which is exactly why losing it hurts both sides.

**Business examples**:

- Two airlines discounting the same route until neither is profitable
- Two startups burning cash to win the same enterprise deal
- Price wars where matching is each firm's dominant move

**What to do**: Change the payoff structure (make defection costly through a contract or penalty),
convert the situation into a repeated game so reputation and retaliation start to matter, or make a
credible commitment to cooperate. The escape is structural, not a plea for goodwill.

## 3. Nash Bargaining Solution

**When**: Two parties negotiate a deal where both gain from agreement but disagree on how to split
the surplus.

**The structure**: Each player has a BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement). The surplus
is the difference between the deal value and both BATNAs combined. The Nash bargaining solution
splits the surplus based on each player's relative bargaining power, typically equally when power is
symmetric.

**Key insight**: Your BATNA is your real position. Improving your BATNA strengthens you more than
any negotiating tactic.

**Business examples**:

- Negotiating a partnership revenue split
- Term sheet negotiation with an investor
- Acquisition price negotiation

**What to do**: Before negotiating, improve your outside option. Know the other party's BATNA, it
sets the floor for what they will accept. The party with the worse BATNA concedes more.

## 4. Stackelberg Leader-Follower

**When**: One player moves first, the other observes and responds. The first-mover has a structural
advantage, or disadvantage, depending on the game.

**The structure**: The leader chooses a strategy knowing the follower will respond optimally. The
leader works backwards from the follower's response function to choose the move that maximises their
own outcome.

**Key insight**: In quantity competition (Cournot), being the leader is an advantage, you commit to
a quantity and the follower scales back. In price competition (Bertrand), being first can be a
disadvantage, the follower undercuts you.

**Business examples**:

- A market leader setting prices that forces competitors into a price-taker position
- A startup entering a market already occupied by an established player
- Announcing a product roadmap publicly to shape competitor investment decisions

**What to do**: Identify whether you are the leader or follower. If leader: commit credibly and
early. If follower: observe, wait, and exploit the leader's constraints.

## 5. Coordination Game / Focal Points

**When**: All parties benefit from coordinating on the same outcome, but there is no obvious way to
coordinate, especially without communication.

**The structure**: Multiple Nash equilibria exist. The question is which one will be selected.
Schelling's focal point theory: players converge on "salient" solutions, the obvious answer that
stands out without negotiation.

**Key insight**: In coordination games, the winner is often whoever makes their preferred
equilibrium the most salient one.

**Business examples**:

- Platform wars (VHS vs Betamax, iOS vs Android)
- Industry standards, whoever establishes the reference standard wins
- Two companies trying to agree on a joint venture structure without tipping their hand

**What to do**: Make your preferred outcome the focal point through public commitment, industry
lobbying, early adopter lock-in, or simply being first and loud.

## 6. Auction Theory

**When**: Multiple parties bid for a single prize. The mechanism design, how the auction is
structured, determines who wins and at what price.

**Key concepts**:

- **Winner's Curse**: In common-value auctions (where the prize is worth the same to all bidders),
  the winner tends to have the most optimistic estimate and overpays. Optimal bidding requires
  shading your bid downward relative to your estimate.
- **Revenue Equivalence Theorem**: Under certain conditions, all standard auction formats (English,
  Dutch, first-price, second-price) produce the same expected revenue for the seller.
- **Second-price (Vickrey) auction**: Bidding your true value is the dominant strategy. Use this
  when you want to elicit honest valuations.

**Business examples**:

- Bidding on a government contract
- Acquiring a company through a competitive process
- Google Ads / programmatic advertising
- Fundraising when multiple VCs are interested

**What to do**: In first-price auctions, shade your bid below your true valuation. In common-value
settings, shade further to correct for winner's curse. When running an auction (selling), consider
second-price formats to maximise participation and honest bidding.

## 7. Signalling and Commitment Devices

**When**: One party has private information the other wants. Or one party wants to make a threat or
promise credible.

**Signalling theory** (Spence): A signal is credible only if it is costly enough that the other type
cannot profitably mimic it. A cheap signal is worthless, anyone can send it.

**Commitment devices**: Removing your own flexibility makes threats credible. Burning the boats,
signing a public contract, making a public announcement, these constrain your future self and change
the other player's beliefs about what you will do.

**Business examples**:

- Hiring a known investor as a board member signals quality to future investors
- Publishing pricing publicly commits you to not offering secret discounts (credible commitment to
  fairness)
- A startup publicly announcing "we will not sell to [large competitor]" changes acquisition
  dynamics
- Education as a signal of ability (costly, therefore credible, even if it adds no skills)

**What to do**: To send a credible signal, make it costly. To make a threat credible, remove your
option to back down. To evaluate others' signals, ask: could someone who lacks the underlying
quality send this signal at a similar cost? If yes, the signal is noise.

## 8. Screening

**When**: The other side holds private information (their true budget, their real walk-away point,
their quality, their risk appetite). You cannot verify it, and asking directly invites a
self-serving answer.

**The structure**: Screening is the mirror image of signalling. In signalling, the informed party
proves their type at a cost. In screening, the uninformed party designs a choice that makes the
other side reveal their type by which option they pick. You offer a menu, each type self-selects the
option built for it. The textbook case is insurance: offer a high-deductible low-premium plan and a
low-deductible high-premium plan, and low-risk and high-risk customers sort themselves.

**Key insight**: You do not need the other side to tell you the truth. You need to design options so
that the truth is revealed by their choice. The menu does the interrogation for you.

**Business examples**:

- Pricing tiers that separate price-sensitive buyers from value-driven ones
- Vesting schedules and earn-outs that separate committed founders from opportunists
- A demanding trial that the serious buyer completes and the tyre-kicker abandons
- Deal terms a confident counterparty accepts and a bluffing one rejects

**What to do**: When you cannot verify a claim, stop asking and start designing. Offer two or three
options whose relative appeal depends on the hidden variable, then read the choice. Make sure no
single option is attractive to every type, or the menu reveals nothing.

## 9. Repeated Games and Reputation Equilibria

**When**: The same players interact repeatedly over time, and future payoffs matter.

**The structure**: In a one-shot game, defection is dominant. In a repeated game, cooperation can be
sustained as an equilibrium, because the threat of future punishment (retaliation, lost cooperation)
outweighs the short-term gain from defecting.

**Folk Theorem**: In infinitely repeated games, virtually any outcome including full cooperation can
be sustained as a Nash equilibrium, as long as players are sufficiently patient (discount rate is
high enough).

**Reputation effects**: In repeated games with uncertainty about player types, reputation becomes a
strategic asset. Being known as tough, reliable, or fair changes what others expect, and changes
what they do.

**Business examples**:

- Long-term vendor relationships (both sides cooperate because they will interact again)
- A VC's reputation in the founder community (affects deal access forever)
- How a company responds to the first employee who leaves shapes all future departures

**What to do**: In repeated interactions, choose your reputation on purpose. Decide early what you
want to be known for, reliable, tough, creative, and play consistently. The first defection is the
most expensive: it reveals your type.

## 10. Cooperative Game Theory / Shapley Value

**When**: Players can form coalitions and share the surplus. The question is how to divide payoffs
fairly given each player's contribution.

**Shapley value**: A method for fair division based on each player's marginal contribution across
all possible coalitions. Compute the average of what each player adds when they join each possible
subset of the coalition.

**Key insight**: The Shapley value is the uniquely fair solution given four axioms: efficiency (full
surplus distributed), symmetry (equal contributors get equal shares), null player (zero contributors
get zero), and additivity (contributions add up correctly).

**Business examples**:

- Splitting a joint venture profit among three partners with different contributions
- Attribution in a multi-channel marketing funnel (which channel deserves credit for the
  conversion?)
- Equity splits among co-founders with different roles and timing
- Revenue sharing in a platform ecosystem

**What to do**: When designing a coalition or profit-sharing arrangement, compute Shapley values to
ensure the split is defensible and stable. Unstable distributions (where some coalition would be
better off leaving) lead to defection.

## 11. Co-opetition / Value Net / PARTS

**When**: The other party is a rival in capturing value but a partner in creating it. This is most
real markets, not pure war.

**The structure** (Brandenburger and Nalebuff): map the Value Net, the four roles around you.
Customers and suppliers sit on the vertical axis, competitors and complementors on the horizontal. A
competitor makes your offering less valuable when they are present, a complementor makes it more
valuable. The same firm can be both at once. PARTS names the five levers you can change:
Players, Added value, Rules, Tactics, Scope. You are not stuck playing the game as given, you can
change who is in it and what the payoffs are.

**Key insight**: Added value, not BATNA, is the cleaner measure here. Your added value is what the
game loses when you leave it: the total pie with you minus the total pie without you. That number is
the ceiling on what you can capture. Raise your added value, or lower others', instead of only
fighting over a fixed split. And look for complementors on purpose: growing the whole pie can beat
winning a larger slice of a small one.

**Business examples**:

- Two hardware makers who both benefit when the platform's app ecosystem grows, then compete on
  devices
- A standards body where rivals cooperate to grow the category before fighting for share
- A SaaS tool and a consultancy that resell each other into the same accounts

**What to do**: Draw the Value Net before assuming the other party is purely an enemy. Identify your
complementors and invest in their success. Then run PARTS: can you add a player to shift the balance,
raise your own added value, change a rule, alter a tactic, or rescope the game? Changing the game
beats playing the existing one well.

## Further frameworks (slated for v1.1)

These are on the bench, named but not yet wired into the six-phase procedure. Reach for them when the
situation fits, and expect fuller treatment in a later version.

- **War of attrition**: both sides bleed cost every period until one quits. Distinct from the
  prisoner's dilemma (here the damage is the waiting itself) and from an escalation lock (no single
  cliff, just a slow drain). The question is who holds the deeper reserves and the higher pain
  threshold, and whether you can credibly signal you will outlast them. Examples: a price standoff
  with no clear end, two startups burning runway for one niche, drawn-out litigation.
- **Chicken / brinkmanship**: two pure equilibria, each asymmetric (one swerves, one holds). The
  whole game is the credibility of your commitment not to swerve. Whoever convinces the other that
  they will hold firm wins, but mutual refusal is the worst outcome for both. Examples: a
  strike deadline, a funding round where each side threatens to walk, geopolitical standoffs.
- **Principal-agent (moral hazard, adverse selection)**: one party acts on behalf of another whose
  interests diverge and whose monitoring is imperfect. Moral hazard is hidden action after the deal
  (the agent slacks). Adverse selection is hidden type before the deal (you hire the wrong sort). The
  fix is contract design: align incentives, screen, tie pay to verifiable outcomes. Examples: hiring
  and vesting, earn-outs, SLAs, equity for contractors.
- **Bayesian games / belief updating**: players hold private types and act on beliefs about each
  other, updating as moves reveal information. This is the conceptual spine under
  incomplete-information situations. Practitioner level, not formal perfect Bayesian equilibrium:
  track what each move signals about type, and what you should rationally believe after seeing it.
  Examples: inferring a counterpart's budget from their counters, reading whether a rival's
  aggression is strength or bluff.
