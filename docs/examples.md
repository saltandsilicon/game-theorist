# Worked Examples

Four real-world situations modelled through the six phases.

---

## 1. SaaS pricing war

**Situation**: You run a B2B SaaS at $200/month. A competitor just dropped their price to $120. You
have better retention and a stronger brand. They have cheaper infrastructure. You have been growing
15% MoM. They appear to be burning cash.

### Phase 1: Deconstruction

Game type: **sequential, repeated, mixed-motive** (zero-sum on market share, positive-sum on
category growth).

You moved first historically (established $200 as the anchor). They responded with $120, a
Stackelberg follower move. They are now signalling a willingness to enter price war territory.

Key asymmetry: they have cheaper infrastructure (lower floor), you have stronger retention (higher
LTV justifies higher CAC).

### Phase 2: Incentive Mapping

**Competitor**:

- Stated: compete on price
- True: probably acquiring users to hit a growth metric for their next raise
- BATNA: continue burning cash at current rate (bad, time pressure is high)
- Time horizon: short (funding deadline)

**You**:

- Stated: maintain price integrity
- True: protect LTV:CAC ratio while not ceding market
- BATNA: hold price and accept slower growth (acceptable given profitability)
- Time horizon: long

### Phase 3: Strategy Space

Your options: match price, ignore, introduce a lower tier, attack on value (not price).

Dominant strategy analysis:

- Matching $120 is dominated, you destroy margin, erode brand positioning, and they can go lower
  given their infrastructure advantage
- Ignoring risks losing price-sensitive prospects but preserves your segment
- Lower tier (e.g., $99 for a stripped product) creates price discrimination without cannibalising
  the core

### Phase 4: Equilibrium

If you hold at $200 and they hold at $120: **stable segmentation**. You own the quality segment,
they own the price segment. Both survive. This is the cooperative equilibrium, two distinct market
positions.

If you drop to match: **price war spiral** likely. They can go lower. You end up in a race to their
floor, not yours.

### Phase 5: Recommendation

**Primary**: Hold $200. Launch a $99 "Starter" tier with meaningful limitations (seat cap, no API,
limited integrations). This is price discrimination, not a concession. It captures price-sensitive
buyers without signalling that your core product is worth $99.

**Commitment device**: Announce the Starter tier publicly. This makes it credible that $200 is the
real product, not an inflated number you will negotiate down.

**Signal**: Write a public post explaining your pricing philosophy (quality, retention, ROI). This
moves the conversation from price to value, and makes the competitor's $120 read as cheap rather
than smart.

**Contingency**: If they drop below $80, they are burning cash to acquire unprofitable customers.
Let them. Their time horizon is shorter than yours.

## 2. Investor term sheet, two VCs interested

**Situation**: You have term sheets from two VCs. VC-A offered $3M at $12M pre. VC-B offered $2.5M
at $10M pre. You want VC-A's network but VC-B has a better founder reputation. Both know the other
is in the process.

### Phase 1: Deconstruction

Game type: **auction** (you are the seller, they are the bidders). It is not a pure price auction,
you are also bidding for their quality (board behaviour, network, follow-on).

Sequential information: both know they are competing. This changes their behaviour, each faces a
winner's curse risk (overpaying to win a deal they want).

### Phase 2: Incentive Mapping

**VC-A**:

- True objective: win the deal (they reached out first, they have more to prove)
- BATNA: deploy capital elsewhere (not urgent, they have a fund to deploy)
- Risk tolerance: moderate (institutional fund, LP accountability)

**VC-B**:

- True objective: maintain reputation as founder-friendly (this deal is also marketing)
- BATNA: next deal in pipeline (also not urgent)
- Time horizon: 7-10 year fund cycle, longer than yours

**You**:

- True objective: best partner, not highest valuation
- BATNA: bootstrap another 3 months (not comfortable but real)

### Phase 3: Strategy Space

Run a formal process (tight deadline, all bids due same day) vs negotiate bilaterally vs accept best
existing offer now.

Formal process (second-price auction dynamics): forces both to bid close to their true valuation.
Reduces winner's curse. You get honest offers.

Bilateral: lets you extract more from one, but risks poisoning both relationships if they compare
notes later.

### Phase 4: Equilibrium

With bilateral negotiation and both knowing about each other: **coordination game**. The focal point
is whoever moves fastest. First to improve terms wins, but risks overpaying (from their perspective)
or underselling (from yours).

### Phase 5: Recommendation

**Primary**: Set a deadline. "We are making a decision by Friday. If you want to revise terms, now
is the time." This creates urgency without a formal process, and signals you have a real
alternative.

**Signal to VC-A**: name the network value you need. Make them feel chosen for reasons beyond
valuation. This activates non-price competition, they offer value-adds, introductions, references.

**Signal to VC-B**: name the founder reputation angle. "We have heard great things from [founder
X]." This reinforces that you are evaluating them on non-price dimensions too.

**Commitment device**: Tell each party you will decide by Friday and will not reopen after. Stick to
it. Credibility here compounds into how they treat you post-investment.

## 3. Key employee counter-offer

**Situation**: Your head of engineering just received an offer from a competitor, $40K more, fully
remote. They told you directly. They have been with you 3 years. You can match the salary but not
the full remote flexibility (you have a 3-day in-office policy you are trying to maintain).

### Phase 1: Deconstruction

Game type: **bargaining** (Nash bargaining solution). Both sides gain from agreement (you keep
institutional knowledge, they keep seniority and equity vesting). You have a structural constraint
they don't.

One-shot or repeated: this interaction is one-shot in form, but your company's response sets a
precedent for every future counter-offer (repeated game externality).

### Phase 2: Incentive Mapping

**Employee**:

- Stated: wants $40K more and full remote
- True: possibly feeling undervalued or seeking change. Money and remote are the rationalisation,
  not always the cause
- BATNA: take the other offer (credible, they told you about it)
- Time horizon: immediate financial gain vs vesting cliff, equity upside, relationship

**You**:

- Stated: want to retain them
- True: want to retain AND not set a precedent that leaving triggers a raise
- BATNA: hire a replacement (6-12 month ramp, knowledge loss, expensive)

### Phase 3: Strategy Space

Match fully, match partially, offer non-salary compensation, let them go, negotiate on remote
flexibility.

Dominated strategies:

- Making them feel guilty for having the offer: dominated by any other approach
- Matching exactly: signals that you only respond to threats (trains future behaviour)

### Phase 4: Equilibrium

If you match in full: stable, but you have set a precedent. Anyone who wants a raise now knows the
playbook.

If you don't match: they leave, and the precedent is "we don't negotiate", which has its own signal
to other employees.

### Phase 5: Recommendation

**Primary**: Match the salary AND reframe the remote policy as negotiable for them, framed as a
performance/trust agreement ("based on your track record, we are offering you flexible arrangements
that we are not offering broadly").

**Why**: The salary match signals you value them. The individualised remote arrangement signals it
is merit-based, not a policy change, which limits the precedent.

**Commitment device**: Document the arrangement formally. This makes it feel earned, not extracted.

**Contingency**: If they still leave, do the exit interview seriously. The real reason is usually
not money. Fix that for the next person.

## 4. Partnership negotiation, revenue share

**Situation**: You are negotiating a distribution partnership. They will promote your product to
their 50K user base. You expect 5% conversion at $200 LTV. They want 30% of revenue on referred
customers. You think 15% is fair. You have one other potential partner at worse terms.

### Phase 1: Deconstruction

Game type: **bargaining** with coalition dynamics. Both gain from the deal (positive-sum). The
question is how to split the surplus.

Key variable: who needs the deal more?

### Phase 2: Incentive Mapping

**Partner**:

- True objective: recurring revenue from their existing asset (the user base)
- BATNA: promote a competitor's product instead (real threat, they have alternatives)
- Time horizon: wants recurring income, not one-time

**You**:

- True objective: distribution at low CAC
- BATNA: other partner at worse terms + paid acquisition (more expensive)
- Constraint: if LTV is $200 and CAC via paid is $80, you can afford up to 40% rev share and still
  be better off than paid acquisition

### Phase 3: Strategy Space

The surplus: 50,000 users x 5% conversion x $200 LTV = $500K potential value. At 30% they get $150K.
At 15% they get $75K. Your BATNA costs you ~$80 CAC = $200K in acquisition costs for the same 2,500
customers.

You can afford up to 40%. 30% is within your range, you don't need to say that.

### Phase 4: Equilibrium

Nash bargaining: split the surplus relative to BATNAs. Their BATNA (promote a competitor) is
credible. Your BATNA (alternative partner + paid) is more expensive than 30%. The equilibrium sits
closer to 25-30% than 15%.

### Phase 5: Recommendation

**Primary**: Counter at 18%, frame it as "below-market but with a tiered structure." Offer 18% base,
25% above $50K quarterly revenue. This gives them upside without locking you into 30% on the first
dollar.

**Commitment device**: Offer a 12-month exclusivity clause in your category in exchange for the
lower base rate. This makes the lower rate read as a trade, not a concession, and limits their BATNA
(they cannot promote competitors while exclusive).

**Signalling**: Share the conversion data you expect. Transparency here creates trust and anchors
their expectations to your model, not their hope.

**Timing**: Close before they pitch this to your competitor. First-mover advantage here is real,
exclusivity changes the game structure entirely.
